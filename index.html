The Silent Emergency: Ocean Acidification and Marine Ecosystems

Adapted for IELTS Academic Reading practice

ASince the beginning of the Industrial Revolution, the world's oceans have absorbed approximately 30 percent of the carbon dioxide released by human activities. While this has significantly moderated the pace of climate change on land, it has triggered a profound chemical transformation beneath the waves. As CO₂ dissolves in seawater, it forms carbonic acid, which subsequently releases hydrogen ions and lowers the pH of the ocean. This process, known as ocean acidification, has already reduced average ocean surface pH from 8.2 to 8.1 — a seemingly modest figure that in fact represents a 26 percent increase in acidity, owing to the logarithmic nature of the pH scale.

BThe ramifications for marine organisms that build shells or skeletons from calcium carbonate are particularly severe. Pteropods — tiny free-swimming molluscs sometimes called "sea butterflies" — provide one of the starkest illustrations. Studies conducted in the Southern Ocean have documented the literal dissolution of pteropod shells in waters that are becoming increasingly corrosive. Since pteropods constitute a critical link in polar food webs, their decline threatens not only specialised predators such as the pink salmon and the mackerel, but also the broader ecosystem stability of entire ocean regions.

CCoral reefs, often described as the rainforests of the sea, face an equally precarious future. Reef-building corals secrete calcium carbonate skeletons, and the rate at which they can do so is directly inhibited by rising acidity. Research published by the Australian Institute of Marine Science indicates that calcification rates on the Great Barrier Reef have declined by more than 14 percent since 1990 — the lowest recorded in at least 400 years. Critically, projections suggest that if atmospheric CO₂ reaches 560 parts per million, the chemical conditions necessary for coral calcification will cease to exist across most of the tropics.

DNot all marine species respond to acidification in identical ways, however. Some organisms appear to benefit, at least in the short term. Certain species of seagrass and phytoplankton, which rely on CO₂ for photosynthesis, have shown enhanced growth rates under elevated CO₂ conditions. This apparent advantage has led some commentators to argue that ocean acidification could stimulate marine primary productivity. Yet ecologists caution that such gains are unlikely to offset systemic losses, particularly because the species most severely harmed — calcifying organisms — occupy foundational roles in oceanic food chains.

EThe economic consequences of acidification extend far beyond the natural world. Global fisheries and aquaculture industries, which employ an estimated 600 million people worldwide and provide the primary source of protein for more than a billion, face mounting risks. Oyster hatcheries along the Pacific coast of North America have already suffered catastrophic larval failures attributed to corrosive upwelling waters. In 2007 and 2008, one major hatchery in Oregon reported losses exceeding 75 percent of its seed oysters, prompting the industry to invest heavily in water chemistry monitoring systems — a cost now routinely passed on to consumers.

FDespite the scale of these disruptions, ocean acidification remains considerably less prominent in public discourse than atmospheric warming. Scientists point to a troubling asymmetry: while average citizens have developed a tangible sense of rising temperatures through lived experience, the chemistry of the deep ocean is largely invisible and intuitively remote. This so-called "perception gap" has tangible policy implications. Funding for acidification research remains a fraction of what is allocated to terrestrial climate science, and international accords have been slow to incorporate explicit targets for ocean chemistry alongside traditional emissions benchmarks.

GSome researchers are investigating localised interventions, including the addition of alkaline minerals such as olivine or limestone to coastal waters to chemically neutralise acidity. Early trials have demonstrated measurable improvements in local pH levels, and proponents argue that, deployed at scale, such approaches could protect vulnerable ecosystems while broader decarbonisation efforts take effect. Critics, however, warn of unforeseen ecological consequences and point out that without concurrent reductions in CO₂ emissions, any chemical buffering would be temporary at best. The scientific consensus remains that mitigating ocean acidification is inseparable from the imperative to reduce atmospheric carbon dioxide.
